## Perspective 1: Quality Compounders
**Representative figures**: Buffett, Munger, Terry Smith  
**Time horizon**: Permanent (10–20+ years)  
**Core question**: Will this company be competitively stronger 20 years from now?  
**Key metric**: ROIC trend, owner earnings, pricing power durability  
**What to look for**:
- ROIC consistently above WACC, widening or stable over time
- Business model that gets stronger with scale (network effects, switching costs, cost advantages)
- Management with skin in the game and long-term capital allocation discipline
- FCF conversion rate >80% of net income over a cycle
- Minimal need for external capital to sustain growth

**Typical blind spot**: Can miss fast-moving platform shifts; may hold too long through moat deterioration

## Perspective 2: Imaginative Growth
**Representative figures**: Baillie Gifford, ARK Invest, Chamath  
**Time horizon**: 5–10+ years  
**Core question**: If everything goes right, how big is the upside? Is this a category-defining company?  
**Key metric**: Revenue growth rate trajectory, TAM expansion, optionality value  
**What to look for**:
- Revenue CAGR >20% with a credible path to sustain for 5+ years
- Multiple embedded options (new markets, new products, platform expansion)
- First-mover or fast-follower in a secular growth market
- Willingness to sacrifice near-term margins for long-term market capture
- Evidence of exponential rather than linear value creation

**Typical blind spot**: Can overpay dramatically; often ignores near-term execution risk and path dependency

---

## Perspective 3: Fundamental Long/Short
**Representative figures**: Tiger Cubs (Chase Coleman, Philippe Laffont), Julian Robertson  
**Time horizon**: 1–3 years  
**Core question**: What is the market missing? What is the Variant View that creates an asymmetric trade?  
**Key metric**: EV/EBITDA vs. peers, earnings revision direction, short interest dynamics  
**What to look for**:
- Identifiable market misunderstanding (consensus wrong on growth, margins, or TAM)
- Earnings revision cycle — is the street cutting or raising estimates?
- Catalyst within 12–18 months that forces repricing
- Clean balance sheet with no hidden liability surprises
- Short thesis: identify what must go right for the bull case and stress-test each assumption

**Typical blind spot**: Mark-to-market risk; great companies can underperform for 2–3 years even if fundamentally right

---

## Perspective 4: Deep Value
**Representative figures**: Seth Klarman, Howard Marks, Benjamin Graham  
**Time horizon**: Patient — 2–5 years until catalyst or mean reversion  
**Core question**: How much would a rational private buyer pay for the entire business today?  
**Key metric**: Replacement cost, liquidation value, normalized earnings power value  
**What to look for**:
- Stock price materially below intrinsic value (≥30% margin of safety)
- Asset-backed downside protection (real estate, patents, cash, brand)
- Cyclical trough earnings creating temporary distortion in standard multiples
- Insider buying at or near current levels
- Catalyst for value realization (spin-off, buyback, activist involvement, industry cycle turn)

**Typical blind spot**: Value traps exist; businesses can be "cheap" because they're structurally declining

---

## Perspective 5: Catalyst-Driven
**Representative figures**: David Tepper, Bill Ackman, Dan Loeb  
**Time horizon**: 6–18 months  
**Core question**: What specific, identifiable event will force the market to reprice this asset?  
**Key metric**: Catalyst timeline specificity, probability of occurrence, magnitude of repricing  
**What to look for**:
- Named catalyst: earnings inflection, regulatory approval, product launch, M&A, spin-off, index inclusion
- Catalyst timeline is concrete and within 12–18 months (not vague "eventually")
- Asymmetric payoff: catalyst realization creates ≥2:1 reward-to-risk
- Options or position sizing strategy that captures the binary outcome efficiently
- What kills the catalyst? Stress-test probability of failure and portfolio impact

**Typical blind spot**: Catalyst delays or failures leave the position without a thesis; timing risk is real

---

## Perspective 6: Macro Tactical
**Representative figures**: Stanley Druckenmiller, George Soros, Ray Dalio  
**Time horizon**: Cycle-dependent (months to years)  
**Core question**: What does the current liquidity, rate, and currency environment imply for this asset?  
**Key metric**: Fed policy direction, credit spreads, USD trend, earnings multiple expansion/contraction  
**What to look for**:
- Rate environment: rising rates compress growth multiples; falling rates expand them
- Liquidity cycle: QE/QT phase and its historical impact on this sector's multiples
- Currency exposure: USD strength headwind for multinationals; tailwind for USD earners
- Positioning: is this sector over- or under-owned by institutions relative to historical norms?
- Macro regime: early cycle (risk-on, growth wins) vs. late cycle (quality/cash flow wins) vs. recession (defense)

**Typical blind spot**: Macro calls are notoriously difficult to time; even correct macro views can be early by 12–24 months
